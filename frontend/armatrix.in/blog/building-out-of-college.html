The Beginning

On October 6, 2023, I found myself in Azerbaijan at the International Astronautical Congress (IAC). Together with two friends, we had won recognition for our work on a trajectory planning algorithm for a robotic arm designed for satellite docking. Standing at booth 38, surrounded by the world's leading aerospace innovators, something clicked.

Making the Decision

The IAC experience validated our concept and sparked a crucial turning point. Rather than pursue a PhD, I decided to build a startup instead. The reasoning was clear: "I wanted to work on my idea, but not through the route of PhD." I had completed three research internships that solidified my preference for independent project work over sponsored academic research.

My friends Ayush and Prateesh, whom I'd known since 2019 as roommates at IIT Kanpur, eventually joined the venture full-time after handling their own placement commitments. The foundation of trust and shared vision built over years of friendship became the bedrock of our company.

The gradCapital Atomic Fellowship

In November, I discovered gradCapital's Atomic Fellowship—a $5,000 grant for early-stage student founders. What followed were three insightful meetings with the gC team within two weeks. By November 29, the trio received their fellowship. The experience felt like "conversations" rather than traditional interviews, with the team exploring our motivation and vision in depth.

This wasn't just about the money—it was validation from people who believed in backing student founders when they're just getting started. The fellowship gave us the runway to prove our concept without the immediate pressure of revenue or profitability.

Building the Basics

With fellowship funding secured, we made our first major investment: a 3D printer for rapid prototyping. This single tool would become crucial for our iterative development process. I graduated early in November, freeing me to focus entirely on the venture while Ayush and Prateesh balanced their final semester.

We registered the company as Armatrix—a name combining "ARM" and "MATRIX" that captured our vision of creating intelligent robotic manipulation systems. We rented an apartment near campus that served dual purposes: our office and our laboratory. Those walls witnessed countless late nights, failed prototypes, and breakthrough moments.

Throughout this period, we visited factories and met with other hardware founders. These conversations were eye-opening. We learned about manufacturing challenges we hadn't anticipated, discovered new application areas for our technology, and refined our understanding of market needs. This led to pivoting our original application toward more refined, market-ready solutions.

By May, all three of us had graduated. We had also exhausted the fellowship funds. But in that time, we had developed our second proof of concept—a significantly more advanced version that demonstrated real commercial potential. We were now positioned to seek pre-seed funding and take Armatrix to the next level.

Looking back, those early months were about more than just building a product. They were about building a company, learning to work as co-founders, understanding our market, and proving to ourselves that this crazy idea of building a robotics company right out of college could actually work.
